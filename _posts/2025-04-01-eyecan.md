## Summary

<p>The purpose of the project, EyeCan, is to support the independence of individuals in the Blind and Visually Impaired (BVI) community by developing a system that helps aging individuals identify and safely navigate heat hazards in a kitchen environment.<br></p>

<figure>
    <img src="/assets/images/eyecan.jpg" alt="EyeCan Test Setup" style="max-width: 100%; height: auto;">
    <figcaption class="caption" style="margin-bottom: 24px;">EyeCan Test Setup at McMaster's Capstone Expo 2025</figcaption>
</figure>

<p><br>This project was created by a team of 5 Mechatronics Engineering students from McMaster University. It was supervised and generously sponsored by Dr. Matthew
Giamou, an Assistant Professor in the Department of Computing and Software at McMaster University.<br></p>

<figure>
    <img src="/assets/images/eyecan_team.jpg" alt="EyeCan Team Photo" style="max-width: 100%; height: auto;">
    <figcaption class="caption" style="margin-bottom: 24px;">EyeCan Team (from left to right): Sabrina Sousa, Corbin Jarrett, Dr. Matthew Giamou, Julia Meeson, Magalie Durepos-Létourneau, and Couper Smith</figcaption>
</figure>

<p><br>EyeCan consists of two subsystems: a primary camera hub that monitors the kitchen area, and a secondary wearable system with haptic feedback devices. The primary system uses a thermal camera to detect heat hazards and a standard camera paired with OpenCV to track the user's hand. When a heat hazard is detected within a defined proximity to the user's hand, the systems communicate using bluetooth low energy (BLE) to trigger haptic feedback on the wearable and alert the user. The vibration frequency increases as a user gets closer to the hazard.</p>
